How Often Should You Mow Your Lawn in the Waikato?

Four Seasons Lawn Services Lawn Care Guide 5 min read

One of the most common questions we get asked — by both homeowners and commercial property managers — is simple: how often should the lawn actually be mowed? In the Waikato, the honest answer is "it depends on the season," and it changes more than most people expect.

Waikato grass doesn't grow at a steady rate all year. Matching your mowing round to the season is the easiest way to keep a lawn looking sharp without overdoing it.

Spring (September to November)

This is when Waikato lawns wake up properly. After winter, warmer soil and longer days push grass into its fastest growth of the year. A lawn that was fine on a monthly cut over winter can easily need mowing every one to two weeks once spring gets going. Letting growth run ahead of you here means a harder, messier cut later — so this is the season to get ahead of it early.

Summer (December to February)

Growth stays strong through summer, particularly after rain. Weekly to fortnightly mowing keeps most home lawns looking tidy, though this can slow slightly during dry spells. It's also a good time to keep the mower height a touch higher than usual — a slightly longer cut helps grass handle heat and dry patches better than scalping it short.

Autumn (March to May)

Growth starts easing off as the days shorten, but autumn brings its own job: leaf fall and general garden tidy-ups. Mowing can usually stretch back out to every two to three weeks, often combined with a garden clean-up and green waste removal before things slow right down for winter.

Winter (June to August)

Growth is at its slowest, and for a lot of Waikato lawns, monthly (or even less) is plenty. This is also a reasonable time to focus on other jobs — hedge shaping and garden bed prep — so everything's ready to go once spring growth kicks back in.

A few general rules of thumb

  • Never cut more than about a third of the grass blade length in one go — cutting too short too fast stresses the lawn
  • Raise the mower height slightly during hot, dry stretches
  • Sharp mower blades matter as much as timing — a blunt blade tears grass rather than cutting it cleanly
  • If you're ever unsure, it's better to mow a little early than let it get away on you — an overgrown lawn takes longer, costs more, and stresses the grass more than staying ahead of it
